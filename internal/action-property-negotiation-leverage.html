Why $925,000 Is the Right Number

The Logic Behind the Recommended Offer

  1. Shows Good Faith Without Caving A $25,000 move signals that you are serious and willing to negotiate, but you are not bidding against yourself. It tells the seller: this buyer is reasonable, engaged, and informed.
  2. Preserves the Remediation Argument At $925,000, the remediation cost ($15K–$45K) still justifies a seller credit or seller-funded repair. Your net effective cost stays in the $880K–$910K range — right where the first offer was.
  3. Creates Room for One More Round If the seller counters at $950K–$960K, you can split the difference and land around $940K with concessions. You have given yourself two moves instead of one.
  4. Pressures With Facts, Not Emotion Attach the remediation estimates and inspection report to the offer. Let the documentation do the heavy lifting. The seller's agent sees the same numbers you do — and knows the next buyer will see them too.
  5. Include a Deadline Set a 5-business-day response window. Urgency prevents the seller from sitting indefinitely. If no response, the offer expires and you are free to walk — or re-engage at a lower number after more time on market.

Suggested Talk Track

“We appreciate the seller's consideration of our initial offer. After reviewing the inspection findings and obtaining independent remediation estimates, we remain very interested in the property at a price that reflects its current condition. We are prepared to increase our offer to $925,000, with the understanding that the seller will provide a remediation credit of $35,000 at closing to address the documented water intrusion. This is a fair offer that accounts for the work required and protects both parties. We would appreciate a response within five business days.” — Framework for agent or direct communication

Active Leverage Points

Water Intrusion — Material Defect
High
Documented water intrusion is a material defect under Alabama real estate law. Remediation costs for a 7,098 sq ft property range from $15,000 to $45,000+ depending on scope. This is a dollar-for-dollar justification for a price reduction.
Action: Attach contractor estimates to the counter-offer. Let the numbers speak for themselves.
Seller Must Disclose to Every Future Buyer
High
Alabama law requires disclosure of known material defects. The seller cannot reject your offer and find an uninformed buyer. Every future buyer will see the same inspection findings and make the same argument.
Action: This is your strongest silent leverage. You do not need to say it explicitly — the seller's agent already knows.
Insurance & Lending Risk
High
Properties with known water intrusion face higher insurance premiums or coverage denials. Lenders may require remediation before loan approval. This is not just your problem — it is a closing risk for the seller with any buyer.
Action: If financing, mention that your lender's appraiser may flag the issue and require remediation as a condition of closing.
Time on Market Works Against the Seller
Moderate
Every week this property sits with a known defect on the disclosure, the seller's position weakens. Price reductions become expected. You are a willing, qualified buyer at a fair price — the seller's best outcome is to negotiate with you now.
Action: Be patient. If the counter-offer is rejected, wait 2–3 weeks before re-engaging at a lower number.
Hidden Damage Risk
Moderate
Visible water intrusion is often the beginning. Elevated moisture behind walls can indicate mold, wood rot, and foundation compromise. The full remediation scope may exceed initial estimates — and that risk premium justifies additional reduction.
Action: Request invasive moisture testing if elevated readings exist. Do not cap your negotiation at visible damage alone.
Rejection Reveals Seller's Expectations
Moderate
The rejection itself is information. If the seller rejected without a counter-offer, they believe they can do better. If they countered, you know their floor. Either way, you now have a data point to calibrate your next move.
Action: If no counter was provided, your $925K offer with deadline forces one — or forces a decision.

Risks to Monitor

!
Competing Offer from Cash Buyer
A cash buyer may accept the property as-is at a higher price, bypassing the remediation argument. Mitigate by staying engaged and responsive — do not let silence drag beyond the recommended timeline.
!
Seller Completes Cosmetic Repair
The seller may attempt a quick, low-cost fix and relist at a higher price. Insist on professional remediation with documentation and warranty from a licensed contractor. Cosmetic fixes do not eliminate the underlying defect.
!
Remediation Costs Exceed Estimates
If invasive testing reveals worse damage, costs could exceed the $45K estimate. Your walk-away threshold of $950K with full credit protects against this — but be prepared to re-evaluate if scope changes significantly.
!
Emotional Attachment Inflates Price
If you love the property, the seller's agent may sense it and hold firm on price. Keep communications factual and professional. Express interest in the property, not attachment to it.
